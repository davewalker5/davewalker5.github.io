One of the pleasures of travel is that it occasionally reconnects food with place.

At home, it is easy for meals to become detached from their origins. Ingredients arrive neatly packaged and labelled. We know, intellectually, that they came from somewhere, but the connection between landscape and plate is often distant and abstract.

Mallorca has a way of making those connections visible again.

Yesterday, sitting beside the harbour in Puerto de Sóller after arriving by boat from Sa Calobra, I ordered calamares a la plancha and a glass of local Sauvignon Blanc. It was one of those lunches that seems perfectly matched to its surroundings: squid from the sea, grilled simply, eaten within sight of fishing boats and the Mediterranean itself.

The old wooden tram rattled occasionally along the promenade, announcing its presence with a gentle whistle. The harbour shimmered in the afternoon heat. The meal felt less like a restaurant dish and more like an expression of the place itself.

The same thought returned that evening over dinner.

One of my favourite Mallorcan dishes is _frito mallorquín_. Historically a dish of thrift and practicality, it combines potatoes, peppers, onions and offal into something far more satisfying than the ingredient list might suggest.

The liver is particularly interesting.

In Britain I have never really learned to enjoy liver. Prepared in the traditional British style, it often remains the dominant flavour of the dish. In Mallorca it feels entirely different. Cut into smaller pieces, cooked quickly, and combined with aromatic vegetables and herbs, it becomes part of a broader composition rather than the sole focus. The result is rich, savoury and deeply satisfying.

It is a reminder that ingredients are only part of a cuisine. The methods and traditions matter just as much.

For the main course I chose _cabrito_ — young goat.

Goat is not a meat encountered very often in Britain outside specialist butchers or certain culinary traditions. Here, however, it feels entirely at home. Prepared simply, the flavour is delicate and distinctive without being overpowering.

As I ate, I found myself thinking back to the afternoon.

Earlier in the day we had travelled through the _Sierra de Tramuntana_, watching goats picking their way across rocky slopes and scrambling over ground that seemed almost impossibly steep. They appeared perfectly adapted to the landscape, as much a part of the mountains as the pines and stone terraces.

Hours later, sitting at a restaurant table, there was a brief moment of connection between those animals and the meal before me. Not discomfort but more an awareness.

The goat on the plate had come, directly or indirectly, from the same landscape through which we had travelled. The meal was not separate from the place. It was another expression of it.

That thought seems to capture something I have been noticing throughout this holiday.

- The butterflies feeding in the flowering tipuana trees
- The gulls wheeling above the harbour
- The Canadair aircraft scooping water from Pollensa Bay before heading south towards a wildfire
- The squid arriving from the surrounding sea
- The goats inhabiting the mountain slopes
- The dishes served on restaurant tables

None of these observations are isolated. They are all connected by the same landscape.

Travel often encourages us to seek out attractions and landmarks. Yet some of the most memorable moments come from noticing these quieter relationships: how geography shapes wildlife, how wildlife shapes culture, and how culture eventually finds its way onto a dinner plate.

Perhaps that is one reason local food can be so rewarding - at its best, it is not merely something to eat, it is a way of understanding a place.

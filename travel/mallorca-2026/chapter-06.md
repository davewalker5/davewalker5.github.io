Some observations announce themselves quietly. Others arrive low over a bay trailing spray.

For several days in Puerto de Pollensa I had noticed bright red and yellow aircraft crossing the sky. At first I assumed they were training. Mallorca, after all, is no stranger to firefighting aircraft, and from a distance it was difficult to tell whether they were exercising or responding to a real incident.

Then, one afternoon, I watched one of them descend towards the water.

The aircraft settled onto the surface of the bay with surprising delicacy for something so large, throwing up a widening plume of spray behind it. For a few seconds it skimmed across the sea, gathering water into its tanks, before lifting away again and climbing steadily southwards towards the mountains.

Only afterwards did I discover that I had been watching one of Spain’s famous water bombers: a Canadair CL-215T operated by Grupo 43 of the Spanish Air and Space Force.

The aircraft carry distinctive markings. On the fuselage side I could make out what appeared to be “43 O 23” — the red roundel of the Spanish Air Force separating the unit number from the aircraft’s individual identity. In reality the aircraft was marked 43-23, one of the long-serving members of the fleet.

Like many aircraft that become part of our mental landscape while travelling, its story turned out to be more interesting than I first expected. Built in 1990 as a Canadair CL-215 and later upgraded to CL-215T standard with turboprop engines, it remains an active working aircraft more than three decades later. While holidaymakers sat beneath parasols and wandered along the promenade, this machine was quietly preparing for a very different purpose: protecting forests, mountains and communities from wildfire.

There was something satisfying about seeing such a specialised aircraft operating exactly as intended. The water scoop itself is a remarkably elegant piece of engineering. Rather than returning to an airfield to refill, the aircraft can collect thousands of litres of water directly from the sea, transforming a sheltered bay into a temporary runway and reservoir in a matter of seconds.

Watching it work also felt distinctly Mediterranean. The blue water, the distant outline of the Tramuntana Mountains, the summer heat, and the occasional passage of a water bomber skimming across the bay all combined into a scene that would have seemed improbable at home in Oxfordshire.

Travel often encourages us to look for grand sights: cathedrals, mountain ranges, museums and monuments. Yet some of the most memorable observations are unexpected. A gull, a moth, an unfamiliar tree, or an aircraft carrying out a task that is entirely ordinary to local people but extraordinary to a visitor.

That afternoon the Canadair became part of the landscape of Pollensa Bay. A flash of red and yellow against the blue sea, disappearing southwards with a load of water and a job to do.

Sometimes a field note begins with a question. In this case it began with a plume of spray across the bay.

There are certain smells that seem to bypass thought entirely.

No effort is required. No conscious recollection. A scent arrives, and suddenly a place, a season, or a moment from years ago returns in full colour.

For me, one of those scents is _Piz Buin_ suncream!

It is not a smell I encounter very often in everyday life. In Oxfordshire, far from beaches and holiday resorts, it remains largely absent. Yet open a bottle and the years seem to collapse. Within seconds I am transported back to Mediterranean promenades, hotel balconies, sea views, and long afternoons spent wandering unfamiliar streets in bright sunshine.

I was reminded of this while sitting in Puerto de Pollença on Mallorca.

The bottle itself has changed little over the years. Brown plastic, orange sun logo, white lettering. Familiar enough that it feels almost timeless. More than once during this holiday I have found myself catching the scent and realising that what I was experiencing was not simply sunscreen, but memory.

The smell forms part of a wider tapestry of associations.

Salt carried on a warm sea breeze.

Fresh coffee from a café overlooking the bay.

Sun-heated stone and pavement.

Pine resin and dry Mediterranean vegetation.

The faint scent of hotel foyers, beach towels, and luggage that has travelled a long way.

None of these smells are remarkable on their own. Together, however, they create something much greater than the sum of their parts. They become a place.

This holiday has already accumulated its own collection of sensory memories.

Painted Lady butterflies feeding high in flowering trees along the seafront.

A large palm moth resting quietly on a wall before revealing unexpected colours when disturbed.

The continuous passage of yellow and red firefighting aircraft scooping water from the bay.

Conversations over coffee about language, history, architecture, and the distinctive character of the Balearic Islands.

These observations are easy to record in a notebook. Dates, locations, species names, and photographs all have their place.

The smells are harder.

Natural history often concerns itself with what can be counted, measured, identified, or classified. Yet our experience of a place is built from far more than that. It includes sound, temperature, light, texture, and scent. These elements are rarely captured directly, yet they often prove to be the most enduring.

Years from now I may struggle to remember which day a particular butterfly was seen, or precisely where a photograph was taken.

I suspect, however, that the smell of Piz Buin will still be capable of carrying me straight back to this Mallorcan summer.

A simple bottle of suncream, and with it an entire season.

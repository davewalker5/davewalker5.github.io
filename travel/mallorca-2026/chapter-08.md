There is something faintly melancholy about the final morning of a holiday.

Not the journey home itself. Not even leaving a place you have enjoyed. Rather, it is the act of packing.

When we arrive somewhere new, the room is neutral. Pleasant, comfortable, welcoming perhaps, but anonymous. It belongs to no-one in particular. The wardrobe is empty. The bedside tables are bare. The bathroom shelf contains only the small collection of hotel toiletries provided for the next guest.

Then, gradually and almost without noticing, we move in.

A book appears beside the bed. Sunglasses are left on the table. Chargers snake their way across a desk. Cameras, hats, notebooks, water bottles and the assorted debris of daily life find temporary homes of their own. The room acquires routines and habits. It begins to feel familiar.

For a week or two, this corner of Mallorca becomes part of everyday life.

This morning, as I packed the suitcase, I realised that the process is quietly reversed.

The book disappears first. Then the charger. The bathroom shelf is cleared. The wardrobe empties. One by one, the small signs of occupation vanish until the room returns to what it was when we first arrived: a pleasant but anonymous space waiting for somebody else.

The room forgets us remarkably quickly.

Perhaps that is why the final morning always feels different from the rest. The holiday is not quite over. There may still be breakfast to enjoy, a final walk along the promenade, one last coffee beneath the trees. Yet packing has already begun the process of departure. We are no longer fully inhabiting the place. We are slowly withdrawing from it.

As I looked around our room in Puerto de Pollensa this morning, stripped once again of books, cameras and the familiar clutter of travel, it struck me that there is an interesting contrast here with the natural history observations that fill so much of these Field Notes.

Natural history often begins with traces: a footprint in mud, a feather caught on a fence, a shell washed onto a beach, a bird heard but not seen. Evidence that something passed this way.

Packing is the opposite. It is the deliberate removal of traces.

And yet the process is not entirely one-sided.

The room returns to neutrality, but we do not.

The painted ladies feeding high in the flowering tipuana trees. The Audouin’s gulls over the bay. A palm borer moth resting in the evening warmth. Canadair water bombers scooping seawater from Pollensa Bay. Calamares a la plancha in Port de Sóller. Flamenco rhythms carried by dancing feet. Conversations over glasses of local wine as the sun slipped behind the mountains.

Those things have already been packed away long before the suitcase is closed.

The room forgets us.

We carry the place home.

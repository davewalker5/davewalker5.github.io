# Morphological Examples

The examples presented here, and included as presets with the shell generator, explore a small region of shell morphospace generated by the computational growth models described in the preceding chapters.

Although inspired by real shells, these examples are not intended as reconstructions of particular species. Instead, they serve as computational experiments that investigate how growth processes, geometry, and developmental constraints interact to produce recognisable shell forms.

Across the examples, a common theme emerges: large differences in morphology often arise from relatively small changes in growth rules. Altering the growth trajectory, aperture geometry, shell expansion rate, coiling behaviour, chamber structure, or ornamentation can transform one shell architecture into another while retaining the same underlying computational framework.

The examples are organised into several broad morphological families.

## Coiled Nautiloids

These forms explore chambered logarithmic shells inspired by nautiloids. Their tightly coiled geometry illustrates the classical relationship between logarithmic growth, chamber formation, and shell expansion.

## Ammonoids

These examples build upon the chambered coiled architecture but introduce alternative coiling styles and stronger ornamentation. Forms such as the ammonite, serpenticone, and crioceratite demonstrate how changes in shell expansion and whorl interaction can produce dramatically different morphologies while preserving the same underlying developmental principles.

## Gastropods

The gastropod-inspired forms emphasise aperture-driven growth and shell architecture rather than internal chambering. Low-spired, high-spired, and flared forms illustrate how shell shape can vary through changes in coiling geometry, aperture orientation, and shell expansion.

## Orthoconic and Curved Nautiloids

These examples depart from coiled growth entirely. Instead of following a logarithmic spiral, the shell develops along a centreline growth path, producing straight or gently curved chambered shells. The orthocone, annulated orthocone, and cyrtocone demonstrate how substantial morphological diversity can emerge from modifications to growth trajectory while retaining the same fundamental chambered shell architecture.

Taken together, these examples illustrate a central observation of computational shell morphology: shell form is governed less by ornamentation than by growth architecture. Features such as coiling strategy, growth trajectory, aperture geometry, chamber spacing, and whorl interaction exert a far greater influence on overall morphology than surface detail alone.

The examples therefore represent not merely a collection of shell models, but a small computational atlas of shell morphospace, exploring how diverse biological forms can emerge from a shared set of geometric growth processes.

# Serpenticone Ammonite-Like Shell

{% assign assets_path = site.assets_url | append: "/images/modelling/shells/" %}
{% include image-block.html images=page.images assets=assets_path %}


The serpenticone ammonite-like preset explores an evolute ammonoid morphology characterised by slow shell expansion, a broad open umbilicus and strongly visible earlier whorls.

Unlike the more tightly coiled ammonite preset, successive whorls overlap only modestly, allowing much of the shell’s growth history to remain visible. The resulting form resembles serpenticone ammonites, a group of planispiral ammonoids distinguished by their open coiling and relatively gradual whorl expansion.

Characteristics:

- Broad open umbilicus
- Slow shell expansion
- Strongly evolute coiling
- Visible earlier whorls throughout growth
- Moderate radial ribbing
- Chambered internal structure
- Visible siphuncle following the spiral path

The resulting morphology occupies an intermediate position between tightly coiled ammonites and the more openly coiled crioceratite forms. Although the shell remains fully planispiral, the reduced expansion rate produces a markedly different appearance and highlights the influence of growth parameters on overall shell architecture.

From a modelling perspective, the serpenticone provides a particularly clear demonstration that substantial morphological variation can emerge without introducing a new growth framework. The shell is generated using the same logarithmic spiral model as the ammonite preset, with the distinctive form arising primarily from changes in shell expansion, whorl overlap and ornamentation.

The preset illustrates how relatively small adjustments to a handful of parameters can transform a conventional ammonite-like shell into a recognisably different cephalopod morphology. In this sense, the serpenticone occupies an important intermediate region of morphospace between tightly coiled ammonites and the partially uncoiled heteromorph forms represented by the crioceratite.

Transparent rendering additionally reveals:

- Internal chamber spacing
- Septal placement
- Siphuncle trajectory
- The effect of reduced shell expansion on the visibility of internal structures

providing a simplified visualisation of ammonoid shell architecture and demonstrating how changes in external form can occur while preserving the underlying chambered cephalopod construction.

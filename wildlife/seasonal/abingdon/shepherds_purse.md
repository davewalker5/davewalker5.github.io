Shepherd’s Purse is a small and easily overlooked plant of roadside verges. Its tiny white flowers and distinctive heart-shaped seed pods make it recognisable on closer inspection.

This page summarises how the species appears in the records: the structure of its flowering period across the year.

## Flowering Period

<img src="/wildlife/reports/Year-In-The-Life/year_in_the_life_shepherds_purse_abingdon_totals.png" caption="Monthly totals (flowering records)"/>

Shepherd’s Purse shows a near year-round presence, but with a distinctly structured seasonal pattern rather than uniform continuity.

Records occur in all months, confirming its ability to persist throughout the year. However, activity is not evenly distributed. Instead, the pattern is characterised by two clear peaks:

- A strong spring peak (March–May)
- A secondary autumn–early winter peak (October–December)

These peaks are separated by a pronounced mid- to late-summer decline (July–September), where both presence and sightings drop to their lowest levels.

The overall shape is therefore bimodal, with high activity in the cooler, more favourable parts of the growing season and a suppression during the height of summer.

Interpretation

The flowering pattern of Shepherd’s Purse reflects a flexible but seasonally constrained opportunistic strategy.

The species is able to:

- Establish and flower rapidly in early spring
- Re-establish and flower again in autumn and mild winter conditions
- Maintain a low-level presence even during less favourable periods

However, it shows reduced activity during mid-summer, likely reflecting:

- Increased competition from taller vegetation
- Drier soil conditions
- Reduced suitability of disturbed microhabitats

This results in:

- A dual-peak flowering pattern aligned with favourable conditions
- A summer trough, rather than continuous high activity
- A persistent but variable presence across the year

Like Red dead-nettle, it contributes to the continuity of flowering in the landscape, but not as a flat background signal. Instead, it reinforces seasonal transitions — rising early in spring, retreating in summer, and returning again in autumn.

Overall, the pattern reflects a resilient, opportunistic species with multiple flowering windows, capable of maintaining presence year-round while responding strongly to seasonal constraints.

## Notes

These patterns are derived from long-term personal field records and should be read as descriptions of observed flowering rather than complete biological accounts.

For species such as Shepherd’s Purse, flowering may occur across a wide seasonal window and is often influenced by local conditions and disturbance. The observed pattern reflects both biological flexibility and the likelihood of recording a small, inconspicuous plant.

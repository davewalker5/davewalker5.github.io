In Puerto de Pollensa, in a restaurant tucked away down a side street just off the seafront, a flamenco performance unfolded. It was clearly a modern interpretation, designed in part for visitors like us. Purists might have found plenty to debate. Yet, as so often happens when travelling, what began as entertainment gradually became something more interesting.

The familiar image of flamenco was there: dramatic poses, flowing dresses, Spanish guitar, and all the visual elements that have become synonymous with the art form. It was enjoyable, certainly, but it was a particular sequence that stopped me paying attention to the spectacle and started me paying attention to the performance.

The dancers began a prolonged passage of footwork.

At first it seemed simply energetic. Then it became apparent that something else was happening. The feet were not merely accompanying the music. They were the music.

The rapid patterns of heel and toe strikes created a dense web of rhythm that interacted with the guitar and handclaps. This was not dancing in the sense I instinctively understood it. It was percussion. The floor itself had become an instrument.

The technical term is _zapateado_: the rhythmic footwork that forms a central part of flamenco. Yet names and definitions only go so far. What struck me was the realisation that I was no longer watching somebody dance. I was listening to a musician who happened to be using their feet.

Perhaps my own interests nudged me towards that interpretation. As someone who enjoys jazz, the performance felt surprisingly familiar. A jazz soloist explores a rhythmic framework, creating variation while remaining anchored to the pulse. This seemed remarkably similar. The dancers maintained the rhythm, embellished it, pushed against it, and returned to it, all while remaining in conversation with the other performers.

The feet became another voice in the ensemble.

Travel often produces moments like this. One arrives with assumptions and categories already formed. A shell is a shell. A bird is a bird. A dance is a dance. Then, for a brief moment, the boundaries blur. The shell becomes mathematics. The bird becomes migration. The dance becomes music.

Flamenco has deep roots and a long history, and what we witnessed in Puerto de Pollensa was undoubtedly shaped by the expectations of a modern holiday audience. Yet recognising that does not diminish the skill involved. If anything, it makes the achievement more impressive. Beneath the theatricality lay years of practice, physical endurance, timing, and musical understanding.

Watching the performance with a carafe of local red wine on the table and the evening settling over the bay, I found myself unexpectedly captivated. Not by the costumes or the choreography, but by the simple and rather remarkable idea that a person could create such intricate rhythms using nothing more than a pair of shoes and a wooden floor.

At some point during the performance, I stopped watching a dancer and realised I was listening to a musician.

And once that thought had occurred, it was impossible not to hear the music in the floorboards.

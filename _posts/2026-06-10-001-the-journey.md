Looking back at the beginning of the year, it is remarkable how much Field Notes Journal has changed.

At that point the site was primarily a collection of generated reports, downloadable spreadsheets, charts and maps. The underlying data and analysis were already present, but there was relatively little interpretation and very little narrative. The site documented observations, but it did not yet tell much of a story.

Six months later, the project feels almost unrecognisable.

The most significant change has not been technical but intellectual. Over the course of the year, the focus has shifted gradually away from simply presenting data and towards understanding what that data might reveal. Wildlife reports acquired commentary and interpretation. Seasonal patterns became ecological questions rather than graphical curiosities. The emphasis moved from counts to meaning.

At the same time, a number of previously separate interests began to converge. Wildlife recording, microscopy, bat acoustics, weather observations, travel journals and computational investigations gradually ceased to feel like independent projects. Instead, they became different facets of a larger endeavour: a personal attempt to understand the natural world through sustained observation.

The phrase "a personal natural history of place" emerged during this period and proved unexpectedly useful. Initially conceived as a strapline, it eventually became a guiding principle. It provided a framework within which many apparently unrelated activities could coexist. Bird records, microscopic investigations, seasonal models and travel journals all became part of the same larger story.

One area that developed particularly rapidly was the study of seasonal ecology. What began as straightforward summaries of wildlife observations evolved into a programme of computational natural history. Seasonal models, species classifications and community analyses revealed patterns that were difficult to see directly in the raw observations. Mathematical models ceased to be abstract exercises and instead became another observational instrument, complementing notebooks, binoculars and field guides.

A similar pattern emerged elsewhere. Bat recordings developed from collections of sound files into investigations of behaviour. Signal processing, pulse extraction and feeding buzz analysis transformed recordings into evidence of ecological activity. The process felt remarkably similar to microscopy: taking something largely hidden from direct perception and developing tools that allow it to be examined.

Perhaps the most unexpected development has been the Computational Shell Morphology project. Beginning with simple logarithmic spirals, the work gradually expanded to generate forms resembling nautiloids, ammonites, tower shells and other shelled organisms. What started as a programming exercise became an exploration of how complex biological forms can emerge from surprisingly simple growth rules. Along the way it evolved into one of the clearest examples of the idea that computation can be used as a tool for natural history.

The publishing side of the project changed substantially as well. Reports gradually became publications. PDF booklets appeared first, followed by EPUB editions. Covers, contents pages, publication metadata and licensing information were added. Material that had once existed as generated reports became something intended to be read from beginning to end.

One particularly satisfying moment came when the Tanzania field journal appeared on a Kindle Paperwhite. The experience was oddly symbolic. Material that had originally existed as notes, observations and generated outputs had become something resembling a book. Not merely a report, but a publication that could be carried, read and revisited in the same way as any other volume on a shelf.

The visual identity of the project matured alongside the content. The name Field Notes Journal, together with the stamp mark, typography and publication formats, gradually established a sense of coherence. The site no longer felt like a collection of outputs gathered in one place. It began to feel like a publication with a distinct voice and purpose.

Behind the scenes, the publication workflow matured considerably. Automated report generation, publication pipelines and cloud-hosted assets made it possible to move from individual reports towards a coherent collection of publications. Yet the technical developments ultimately served a larger purpose: helping observations become something that could be shared, revisited and built upon.

What strikes me most strongly when looking back is that the project now seems to have acquired a point of view.

Earlier versions of the site largely answered the question:

> What have I recorded?

Increasingly, the project is attempting to answer a different question:

> What have I learned?

That shift has changed the character of the work. The observations, charts, maps and datasets remain important, but they now feel less like the destination and more like the tools. The purpose of the project has become clearer: to build, slowly and over many years, a personal natural history assembled from observation, investigation, analysis and reflection.

Field Notes Journal still contains reports. It still contains data, charts, maps and downloads. But it is becoming something more than a repository of information.

At the start of the year, the site contained reports.

Now, it is beginning to feel like a body of work.

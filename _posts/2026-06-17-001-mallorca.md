It began, as many good travel observations do, with lunch.

After the boat journey from Sa Calobra, we arrived in Port de Sóller and wandered into a harbour-side restaurant without much planning. The harbour was busy but not hurried; boats moved gently on their moorings and the waterfront cafés hummed with conversation. Looking down the menu, one dish immediately caught my eye: calamares a la plancha.

I ordered a plate of grilled squid and a glass of Mallorcan Sauvignon Blanc.

For me, squid is one of those foods that is transformed by simplicity. In Britain it often appears battered and deep-fried, pleasant enough but sometimes more notable for its coating than for the squid itself. Here, however, the squid arrived exactly as I hoped it would: quickly cooked on a hot griddle, lightly dressed, tender rather than rubbery, carrying the sweetness and flavour of the sea.

It remains my favourite way to eat squid.

The dish itself prompted a small question. Was calamares a la plancha a specifically Spanish creation, or simply a Mediterranean dish that happened to be served under a Spanish name?

The answer, as is often the case, appears to be both.

The phrase a la plancha is distinctly Spanish, referring to cooking on a hot metal plate or griddle. Yet the underlying idea belongs to a much older and broader Mediterranean tradition. Across Spain, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Turkey and elsewhere, fresh squid has long been grilled, roasted, or cooked over fierce heat and served with little more than olive oil and lemon. The ingredients change little because they do not need to.

Sitting beside the harbour in Port de Sóller, it felt like the kind of meal that fishermen around the Mediterranean would instantly recognise, regardless of language or nationality.

As lunch progressed another feature of the harbour repeatedly caught my attention.

Every so often a wooden tram would trundle along the promenade, its polished bodywork gleaming in the afternoon sun. Rather than the harsh horn of a modern vehicle, it announced its presence with a gentle, almost courteous whistle — a soft _poop-poop_ drifting across the waterfront.

The tram is no tourist replica. The Sóller Tramway has linked the harbour with the town of Sóller since 1913, and watching it pass gives the curious impression of stepping sideways in time. It remains part of everyday life rather than a preserved museum piece. People board it to go about their business, just as they have done for more than a century.

Perhaps that is part of the atmosphere that makes Port de Sóller so appealing.

Modern life is present, certainly. Phones appear on café tables and contactless payments are routine. Yet somehow these things do not dominate. The tram still rattles along its tracks. The harbour remains a working harbour. Lunch is allowed to take its time.

The result is not a place frozen in history, but a place that seems to operate at a different rhythm.

Watching the tram pass while finishing the last of the wine, I found myself thinking that Port de Sóller feels almost as though the twentieth century never quite happened. Not because it lacks modernity, but because it has retained something that many places have lost: a willingness to move at a human pace.

The memory I suspect will remain is not any single sight or landmark. Instead it will be a collection of sensations: grilled squid, cool white wine, sunlight on the harbour, the mountains rising beyond the bay, and the distant sound of a wooden tram giving its polite poop-poop as it makes its way along the promenade.

Some places seem determined to hurry you along.

Port de Sóller appears content simply to let you be there.

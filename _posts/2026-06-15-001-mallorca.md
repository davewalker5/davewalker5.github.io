One of the pleasures of returning to Mallorca after many years away is rediscovering just how much wildlife exists alongside the holiday experience.

This morning, sitting beneath the shade of a flowering tree on the seafront, a Tipuana tipu, at Puerto de Pollença, I became aware of a constant movement high in the branches. Looking more carefully, the canopy was alive with butterflies. Dozens of them drifted through the flowers, settling briefly before moving on to the next cluster of blooms.

At first glance they appeared to be painted ladies, their orange upperwings flashing in the sunlight whenever they opened them. The species is a familiar migrant in Britain, but there was something striking about seeing them here in such numbers. Rather than a solitary individual passing through a garden or meadow, these were part of an active feeding aggregation spread through the upper branches of the tree.

Among them may have been a few Cleopatra butterflies, another characteristic Mediterranean species. At a distance, brief glimpses of orange wings and pale undersides made certainty difficult, but the possibility added an extra layer of interest. It is one thing to see a species in a field guide and quite another to encounter it unexpectedly while drinking a morning coffee.

Later in the day another visitor appeared. Hovering continuously among low flowering shrubs was what was clearly a hawk-moth. The insect never seemed to settle, remaining suspended in front of the flowers as it fed, its wings a blur. The front of the body appeared furry and grey, giving it an almost mammalian appearance in miniature.

A closer view when it briefly opened its wings flashes of orange-red on the trailing edge and black and white near the end of the abdomen. Combined with the behaviour, these markings strongly suggested the Hummingbird Hawk-moth (Macroglossum stellatarum), one of the Mediterranean’s most familiar hawk-moths and a species that occasionally reaches Britain as a migrant.

Later, walking by the harbour, I sighted two larger moths, pictured above, approximately an inch-and-a-half long, settling into a shaded spot on a palm. Black, white and red markings at the end of the abdomen and a striking white flash on each upperwing point to the Palm Moth, _Paysandisia archon_, imported to Europe in the 1990s on imported palm trees from its native South America and first reported in Mallorca in 2003.

The encounters lasted only a few moments, but they were enough in one case to transform an anonymous insect into a named species and in both cases memorable observations. It also served as a reminder that natural history does not always require deliberate expeditions. Sometimes it is simply a matter of paying attention to what is already happening around you.

In the space of a single day, a seafront promenade, a flowering tree and a garden shrub had provided a small but satisfying glimpse into the insect life of a Mediterranean summer.

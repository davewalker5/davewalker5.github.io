During a recent visit to the Oxford University Museum of Natural History, I found myself lingering — as usual — in the central court, saying "hello" to "Stan" (the resident T-Rex!) and spending a good three hours or more happily perusing the _History of Life_, _Evolution_ and _Modern Animals_ displays.

Among the specimens was a prominent panel describing the logarithmic spiral and the extraordinary diversity of forms that can emerge from relatively simple growth rules. One line in particular lodged itself firmly in my brain:

> Mollusc shells illustrate beautifully how diversity can arise from just a few variations on one simple mathematical model, encoded in the genes

Underneath it, in my notebook, I scribbled a very short note to self:

> Not to self - I should model this

Which, as it turns out, was a slightly dangerous thing to write.

What began as a simple attempt to plot logarithmic spirals in Python quickly evolved into a much larger exploration of shell morphology and growth geometry. Starting from the basic spiral equation:

<pre>
r = ae^(b&theta;)
</pre>

the project gradually expanded into a small procedural shell modelling system capable of generating:

- Nautilus-like shells
- Ammonite-like shells
- Ramshorn-like shells
- Flared and open-coiled forms
- Transparent cutaway shells showing internal chambers and siphuncles

All emerging from variations on a relatively small set of growth parameters.

One of the most satisfying aspects of the project has been discovering how much visible structure can emerge from extremely simple rules:

- logarithmic growth
- expanding apertures
- periodic ornamentation
- shell wall thickening
- internal chamber generation

The resulting forms are not intended as strict biological simulations, but rather as explorations of computational natural history — investigating how complex natural forms can arise from relatively simple developmental processes.

And, perhaps most importantly, they are simply beautiful objects in their own right.

After showing some of the generated shell renders to my wife, she observed — entirely correctly — that several of them looked remarkably like Guylian Seashell chocolates.

Which, once noticed, becomes difficult to unsee.

The smoother nautilus forms in particular seem to occupy a curious space somewhere between computational morphology, museum reconstruction, and expensive Belgian confectionery!

What I particularly enjoy about the system is that small parameter changes can produce dramatically different shell morphologies. Smooth nautilus-like shells, ribbed ammonites, flattened ramshorn forms, and open speculative geometries all emerge from the same underlying mathematical framework.

Which, in a sense, brings things neatly back to the museum display that started the whole exercise.

The project can now be found under _Computational Shell Morphology_ in the _Wildlife_ section of the site, together with example shell presets and further discussion of the underlying modelling ideas.

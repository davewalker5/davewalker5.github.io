Twelve years is a surprisingly long time.

Long enough for memories to soften around the edges. Long enough to forget details while retaining an overall impression. Long enough, perhaps, for a place to become partly real and partly reconstructed from memory.

This week we returned to Mallorca for the first time in twelve years.

The destination was Puerto de Pollensa, a place we had visited before, but one that now felt familiar and unfamiliar in equal measure. The broad sweep of the bay was instantly recognisable. The mountains beyond were exactly where memory had left them. The seafront promenade, lined with cafés, restaurants and shade trees, felt comfortingly unchanged.

Yet almost immediately I found myself doing what I always seem to do in a new or newly revisited place: looking more closely.

Within hours of arriving I was paying attention not to hotels or restaurants but to gulls.

There appeared to be two distinct species along the waterfront. One looked reassuringly familiar, broadly similar to the herring gulls of home. The other seemed different, carrying a striking red bill that caught the eye even at a distance. The first afternoon became less about arrival and more about observation.

The same happened with butterflies.

A small orange butterfly drifting through the sunshine prompted an identification exercise that eventually led to Painted Lady, one of those remarkable species whose migrations span continents and seas. It is always satisfying to encounter a familiar traveller in an unfamiliar place.

Even the trees demanded attention. Along the seafront stood rows of distinctive coastal trees whose identity I found myself trying to unravel. In retrospect this should not have been surprising. One of the pleasures of visiting somewhere different is encountering species that are entirely ordinary to local residents but novel to the visitor.

Language began to reveal itself in similar ways.

Mallorca is not simply Spanish. It has its own linguistic identity, and before long I was noticing property names beginning with “Ca’n” and “Ca na”. These turned out to be clues embedded in the landscape, references to houses and families, reminders that place names often preserve fragments of local history and culture.

Even pronunciation became an object of curiosity. The familiar Spanish “ll” sound seems subtly different in Mallorca, reflecting the influence of Catalan and the island’s own linguistic traditions. Small details, perhaps, but they add texture to a place.

One of the more unexpected observations involved aircraft.

Throughout the day bright red-and-yellow flying boats repeatedly crossed the bay. At first it was difficult to tell whether they were training or engaged in active firefighting operations. Watching more carefully eventually revealed the answer: they were indeed scooping water directly from the sea.

The aircraft were members of Spain’s famed 43 Group, operating the Canadair water bombers that have become an iconic sight across the Mediterranean during wildfire season. Their repeated circuits across the bay served as a reminder that beneath the tranquillity of a holiday destination lies a landscape that lives with summer heat and the ever-present risk of fire.

Not every discovery was natural history.

One afternoon coffee was accompanied by a local brandy called Suau. This immediately raised a new question: how exactly is one supposed to pronounce it? Such investigations may not appear in conventional field notebooks, but they seem entirely appropriate in a travel journal.

Likewise, sitting on the seafront with a bottle of San Miguel prompted another line of enquiry. Where was it brewed? The answer, like many things in modern life, turned out to be more complicated than expected. Brands often outgrow their origins, becoming detached from the places that first gave rise to them.

These are small observations. None is individually significant.

Yet together they illustrate something I have come to value about travel.

The most rewarding journeys are not necessarily those that involve grand itineraries or famous landmarks. Often they begin with simple attention: watching birds, identifying butterflies, noticing language, following the path of an aircraft across a bay, or wondering about the history behind a name on a bottle.

After twelve years away, Mallorca is becoming familiar again. But it is becoming familiar not through memory alone. It is becoming familiar through observation.

As ever, the process begins with looking carefully.

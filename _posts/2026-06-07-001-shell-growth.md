Only five days have passed since I published a note about an experiment inspired by a museum display on logarithmic spirals.

At the time I had a handful of shell forms working: a nautilus-like shell, an ammonite-like shell, a ramshorn form and a few speculative variations.

I thought I was more or less finished.

I was, of course, completely wrong.

Since then the project has expanded considerably. What began as a simple exploration of logarithmic growth has become a small procedural shell morphology laboratory capable of generating an increasingly diverse collection of shell forms.

{% include fullwidth-image.html assets=page.assets img=page.contact_sheet %}

The image above looks less like a scientific modelling project and more like a selection box of Guylian Seashell chocolates. As I noted in my first post, my wife pointed the similarity out almost immediately and, as with most such observations, once noticed it became impossible to unsee.

Yet the remarkable thing is that every one of these forms emerges from the same underlying framework.

The shells differ not because entirely different models are being used, but because a relatively small number of geometric parameters are being varied:

- growth rate
- aperture shape
- aperture expansion
- whorl overlap
- axial translation
- ornamentation
- shell wall thickness

Small changes can produce surprisingly large differences in morphology.

A particularly satisfying recent development has been the addition of high-spired gastropod forms inspired by shells such as *Turritella*.

Early attempts looked more like springs than shells. The geometry was mathematically correct, but biologically unconvincing.

The breakthrough came from paying closer attention to how successive whorls interact with one another. Increasing whorl overlap and modifying the orientation of the growing aperture transformed the resulting forms from obvious helices into structures that immediately read as shells.

That turns out to be a recurring lesson in this project.

Surface ornamentation matters.

Pigmentation matters.

Ribs and growth lines matter.

But growth architecture matters more.

The trajectory followed by the growing aperture has a far greater influence on overall form than any decorative feature added afterwards.

This feels oddly reminiscent of natural history itself: many of the most dramatic biological differences emerge not from entirely new rules, but from small modifications to existing developmental processes.

The project has also gained the ability to produce presentation-style renders and growth animations.

Watching a shell emerge from a single expanding aperture is surprisingly satisfying. Structures that appear complicated when viewed as finished objects often reveal themselves to be the result of a relatively simple sequence of repeated geometric operations.

In a sense, the animations make visible what the mathematics is doing.

One of the unexpected pleasures of the project has been discovering that it sits comfortably between several disciplines:

- Mathematics
- Geometry
- Computer graphics
- Palaeontology
- Lalacology
- Natural history

It is not quite a biological simulation.

It is not quite a mathematical exercise.

It is not quite digital art.

Instead it occupies an enjoyable middle ground where all three overlap.

And perhaps that was the lesson hidden in the museum display that started this whole adventure.

A logarithmic spiral is only a simple curve.

But given enough time, and a few carefully chosen variations, it can become a nautilus, an ammonite, a ramshorn, a tower shell...

...or, apparently, a premium Belgian chocolate.

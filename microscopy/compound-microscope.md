# Principles of Compound Microscopes

## Overview

<p align="center">
  <img src="/assets/images/microscope-light-path.png" alt="Microscope Light Path" height="500">
</p>

In a compound microscope, the specimen is illuminated by light that is focused onto it by the condenser, which sits beneath the stage. The condenser gathers light from the illumination source and concentrates it into a cone of light directed through the specimen.

As the light passes through the specimen, it is altered by the specimen’s structure. Some of the light continues straight through, while other portions are scattered or redirected by tiny features within the specimen. These fine structures effectively break the light into multiple diffracted rays.

Just above the specimen sits the objective lens. Its role is to collect as many of these modified rays as possible and bring them together so that they can form a magnified image.

Inside the objective, rays entering at different angles are first gathered by the front elements of the lens system into a circular region known as the aperture, located at the objective’s back focal plane. At this point, the light is arranged according to the angles at which it entered the objective.

The rear elements of the objective then combine these rays. Because the rays arrive at slightly different angles, the lens causes them to overlap and interfere with one another. This pattern of constructive and destructive interference recreates the fine spatial details of the specimen and produces a magnified optical image inside the microscope tube. This image is called the intermediate image.

The eyepiece does not create the image itself. Instead, it acts like a magnifying glass that enlarges the intermediate image so that it can be comfortably observed by the eye.

In summary, a compound microscope works by:

- Illuminating the specimen with a focused cone of light
- Collecting the light modified by the specimen using the objective lens
- Forming a magnified image inside the microscope tube
- Enlarging that image for observation with the eyepiece

A microscope therefore does more than simply magnify an object. It forms an optical image from the light that the specimen transmits or scatters, and it is this image that the observer ultimately examines.

## Numerical Aperture

Numerical aperture is defined as the range of angles over which the objective can accept light and is expressed numerically as:

$$
NA = n \cdot \sin \theta
$$

Where n is the refractive index of the medium (~ 1 for air) and θ is half-angle of the maximum cone of light the objective can accept. For maximum resolving power:

$$
NA_{condenser} \ge NA_{objective}
$$

This ensures the specimen is illuminated from all angles the objective can use but it doesn’t always produce the optimal image for visual examination as it decreases contrast.

Microscopists therefore reduce the cone of illumination slightly to produce stronger contrast and more visible structure and a classic rule taught in teaching labs was that the illumination cone should fill approximately 70-80% of the objective aperture.

## Resolving Power

The power of a microscope to reveal fine detail is determined not solely by magnification but by the resolving power of the objective. This resolving power depends principally upon the numerical aperture, which expresses the capacity of the objective to receive light from the specimen at a range of angles.

An objective possessing a large numerical aperture gathers a wider cone of rays and is therefore capable of separating structures that lie very close together. If the numerical aperture is small, many of the oblique rays carrying information about fine detail fail to enter the objective, and the image appears indistinct. The resolution attainable with the microscope is therefore limited by the optical properties of the objective and the wavelength of the light employed.

The theoretical limit of resolution of a light microscope was described by Ernst Abbe, who showed that the smallest distance between two points that can be distinguished as separate is given approximately by:

$$
d = \frac{\lambda}{2 \cdot NA}
$$

Where d is the minimum resolvable distance, λ is the wavelength of the illuminating light, and NA is the numerical aperture of the objective.

This relationship shows that resolution improves when the numerical aperture is increased or when shorter wavelengths of light are used.
